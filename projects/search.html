Revitalizing the Search Experience
Reducing Drop-off for First-Time Users

Practo invited our agency to participate in a competitive design bid focused on improving first-time user retention. Working as part of a four-member UX team, we analyzed behavioral drop-offs within the symptom-based search experience. Using shared analytics, heatmaps, and internal usability testing, we identified search friction as the primary conversion barrier. Our redesign proposal focused on reducing cognitive load, improving trust signals, and increasing booking confidence.

Heuristic Evaluation

Identifying Usability Violations

I started with heuristic evaluation to identify symptoms of deeper usability issues. These universal principles helped guide my analysis of where the experience breaks down.

Violated

Recognition over Recall

Users had to interpret technical medical terms without context. The system required users to know specialty names instead of recognizing their symptoms.

Violated

Visibility of System Status

Unclear relevance feedback ‚Äî users couldn't tell if their symptom search actually matched the suggested specialties.

Violated

User Control & Freedom

Users were forced into category flows with no easy way to navigate back. The restrictive experience removed user autonomy.

Violated

Flexibility & Efficiency

No shortcuts for experienced users. Everyone had to go through the same tedious category selection process.

2

Exploring the Symptoms and Search Bar Again üôÑ

Upon selecting the instant video call option on the search screen, the app takes the user to an exploratory screen with a search bar to look up symptoms.

The Scenario

On searching for a symptom, such as "Chest Pain," the user will receive results for relevant medical specialties. While some of these specialties may be obvious to the general public, I found not every specialty suggested would be understandable for the users.

The Problem

This could cause confusion for users, who may select the wrong specialty due to a lack of information to justify whether the searched symptom matches the selected specialty.

Consequence

This may lead to choosing the wrong doctor, wasting both time and money of user. Users might drop off at this point because of lack of sufficient information to choose a consultant.

Reflection

Key Learnings

01

Healthcare UX Requires Clarity Over Density

Medical interfaces need to prioritize understanding over feature richness. Users are already anxious about their health ‚Äî design should reduce, not add, cognitive load.

02

Users Think in Symptoms, Not Taxonomy

The gap between user mental models (symptoms) and system structure (specialties) was the root cause of friction. Bridging this gap is the designer's job.

03

Search is Trust Infrastructure

When search works well, users trust the platform. When it fails, they leave. Search isn't just a feature ‚Äî it's the foundation of user confidence.

04

Iteration Through Testing Drives Improvement

The feedback loop of testing ‚Üí learning ‚Üí iterating was invaluable. Throughout the 4-month design bid, multiple rounds of validation made the design significantly better.
